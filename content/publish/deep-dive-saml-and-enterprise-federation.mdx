## What is SAML?

**SAML (Security Assertion Markup Language)** is an open standard for exchanging authentication and authorisation data between parties — specifically between an identity provider and a service provider. It is an XML-based protocol maintained by the OASIS (Organisation for the Advancement of Structured Information Standards) consortium.

To understand SAML, it helps to start with a real-world analogy. Imagine you are visiting a foreign country and you need to access a government building. You do not have a local ID card, but you do have a passport issued by your home country. The foreign government trusts your home country's passport office. When you present your passport, the building security does not need to verify your identity from scratch — they simply verify the passport is genuine (check the seal, the watermark, the expiry date) and then grant you access based on the information it contains. SAML works in much the same way: the **identity provider** is the passport office, the **service provider** is the foreign building, and the **SAML assertion** is the passport itself.

### A brief history

SAML has evolved through three major versions:

*   **SAML 1.0** was released in November 2002. It established the core concepts of assertions and the request/response protocol but had limited adoption due to complexity and gaps in the specification.
*   **SAML 1.1** followed in September 2003 with improvements to interoperability, but it still lacked critical features like the standardised metadata format.
*   **SAML 2.0** was ratified in March 2005 and remains the current version. It was a major overhaul that consolidated SAML 1.1 with two other federation specifications — the Liberty Alliance Identity Federation Framework (ID-FF) and Shibboleth. SAML 2.0 introduced standardised metadata, enhanced bindings, the `NameID` management protocol, and single logout.

When people say "SAML" today, they almost always mean SAML 2.0. That is the version we will focus on exclusively throughout this post.

### Why does SAML still matter?

You might wonder — if we have modern protocols like OpenID Connect, why does SAML persist? Several reasons:

*   **Enterprise inertia**: Thousands of enterprise applications were built on SAML. Migrating is expensive and risky.
*   **Feature completeness**: SAML 2.0 handles complex federation scenarios — attribute mapping, NameID management, single logout — that OIDC only gained support for gradually.
*   **Regulatory compliance**: Many government and healthcare organisations mandate SAML-based federation through compliance frameworks.
*   **Vendor support**: Every enterprise IdP supports SAML. Not all legacy applications support OIDC.

## What problem does SAML solve?

Before federated identity existed, every application maintained its own user database with its own passwords. If your organisation used fifteen different SaaS applications, each employee had fifteen separate credentials. The problems with this approach are well documented:

*   **Password fatigue**: Users choose weak passwords or reuse them across applications.
*   **Onboarding and offboarding friction**: Creating and disabling accounts across dozens of systems is slow and error-prone. A terminated employee might retain access to critical systems for days.
*   **No centralised policy enforcement**: Each application enforces its own password complexity rules, multi-factor authentication requirements, and session policies independently.
*   **Audit fragmentation**: Compliance teams must aggregate authentication logs from every application separately.

**Single Sign-On (SSO)** solves these problems by centralising authentication. The user authenticates once with a trusted identity provider, and that authentication is honoured by all connected service providers. SAML is the protocol that makes this trust relationship possible.

Think of it like a trusted letter of introduction. In centuries past, a dignitary travelling between courts would carry a letter sealed by their sovereign. The receiving court would verify the seal, read the letter's contents (the dignitary's name, title, and authorised activities), and grant access accordingly. The receiving court never needed to independently verify the dignitary's identity — they trusted the sovereign who issued the letter. SAML assertions function as these digitally signed letters of introduction.

## What are the SAML roles?

SAML defines three core roles in every authentication exchange:

**Identity Provider (IdP)** — the authority that authenticates the user and issues assertions. In enterprise contexts, this is typically the organisation's centralised identity system — Microsoft Entra ID (formerly Azure AD), Okta, Ping Identity, or an on-premises ADFS server. The IdP maintains the user directory, enforces authentication policies (passwords, MFA, conditional access), and produces signed SAML assertions that vouch for the user's identity.

**Service Provider (SP)** — the application or service that the user wants to access. The SP does not authenticate users directly. Instead, it delegates authentication to a trusted IdP and consumes the SAML assertion to establish a local session. Examples include Salesforce, ServiceNow, AWS Console, Jira, Confluence, and thousands of other SaaS and on-premises applications.

**Principal** — the human user (or occasionally a system actor) who is attempting to access the service provider. The principal interacts with both the IdP (to authenticate) and the SP (to access the service), but the SAML protocol itself flows between the IdP and SP, typically through the principal's web browser acting as an intermediary.

SAML Roles — Identity Provider, Service Provider, and Principal

### The trust relationship

The foundation of SAML federation is the **trust relationship** between the IdP and the SP. This trust is established out-of-band — before any authentication flows occur — through the exchange of **SAML metadata** documents. The metadata contains:

*   The entity's **Entity ID** — a globally unique identifier (typically a URL)
*   The **endpoints** (URLs) for SSO, SLO, and artifact resolution
*   The **X.509 certificates** used for signing assertions and encrypting data
*   The **supported bindings** (HTTP-POST, HTTP-Redirect, HTTP-Artifact)
*   The **NameID formats** the entity supports

Once both parties have exchanged and configured each other's metadata, the trust relationship is established. The SP trusts that any assertion signed by the IdP's private key is genuine, and the IdP trusts that authentication requests from the SP's configured endpoints are legitimate.

## How does SAML authentication work?

The most common SAML flow is **SP-Initiated SSO** — the user starts at the service provider, is redirected to the identity provider for authentication, and is then returned to the service provider with a signed assertion. Let's trace through the complete flow step by step.

![Sequence diagram showing the SP-Initiated SSO flow with numbered steps between Browser, Service Provider, and Identity Provider](/blog/deep-dive-saml-and-enterprise-federation/inline-2.png)

SP-Initiated SSO Flow

### SP-Initiated SSO — step by step

1.  **User accesses the SP** — The user navigates to the service provider (e.g., `https://app.example.com/dashboard`). The SP checks for an existing session. No session exists.
2.  **SP generates an AuthnRequest** — The SP constructs a SAML `<AuthnRequest>` XML message containing its Entity ID, the Assertion Consumer Service (ACS) URL where the response should be sent, a unique request ID, and optionally the requested NameID format and authentication context class.
3.  **SP redirects the browser to the IdP** — The AuthnRequest is encoded (Base64 + URL encoding for HTTP-Redirect, or Base64 for HTTP-POST) and the user's browser is redirected to the IdP's Single Sign-On Service URL.
4.  **IdP authenticates the user** — The IdP receives the AuthnRequest, validates it, and presents a login page (unless the user already has an active IdP session). The user enters their credentials and completes any MFA challenges.
5.  **IdP generates a SAML Response** — Upon successful authentication, the IdP constructs a SAML `<Response>` containing one or more `<Assertion>` elements. Each assertion is digitally signed with the IdP's private key.
6.  **IdP sends the Response to the SP** — The signed Response is sent to the SP's ACS URL, typically via HTTP-POST (an auto-submitting HTML form in the user's browser).
7.  **SP validates the Response** — The SP verifies the XML signature against the IdP's public certificate, checks the `Destination`, `InResponseTo`, `Conditions` (audience restriction, time validity), and `SubjectConfirmation` data.
8.  **SP establishes a local session** — If validation succeeds, the SP extracts the user's identity (from the `NameID` element) and attributes, creates a local application session, and redirects the user to their originally requested resource.

Here is what the AuthnRequest looks like:

```xml
<samlp:AuthnRequest
    xmlns:samlp="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:protocol"
    xmlns:saml="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:assertion"
    ID="_8e8dc5f69a98cc4c1ff3427e5ce34606fd672f91e6"
    Version="2.0"
    IssueInstant="2025-07-12T09:30:00Z"
    Destination="https://idp.example.com/saml2/sso"
    AssertionConsumerServiceURL="https://app.example.com/saml/acs"
    ProtocolBinding="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:bindings:HTTP-POST">
    <saml:Issuer>https://app.example.com/saml/metadata</saml:Issuer>
    <samlp:NameIDPolicy
        Format="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:nameid-format:emailAddress"
        AllowCreate="true"/>
    <samlp:RequestedAuthnContext Comparison="exact">
        <saml:AuthnContextClassRef>
            urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:ac:classes:PasswordProtectedTransport
        </saml:AuthnContextClassRef>
    </samlp:RequestedAuthnContext>
</samlp:AuthnRequest>
```

And here is a simplified SAML Response:

```xml
<samlp:Response
    xmlns:samlp="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:protocol"
    xmlns:saml="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:assertion"
    ID="_response_abc123"
    Version="2.0"
    IssueInstant="2025-07-12T09:30:05Z"
    Destination="https://app.example.com/saml/acs"
    InResponseTo="_8e8dc5f69a98cc4c1ff3427e5ce34606fd672f91e6">
    <saml:Issuer>https://idp.example.com</saml:Issuer>
    <samlp:Status>
        <samlp:StatusCode Value="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:status:Success"/>
    </samlp:Status>
    <!-- Assertion follows (see next section) -->
</samlp:Response>
```

## How does IdP-Initiated SSO work?

In **IdP-Initiated SSO**, the flow starts at the identity provider rather than the service provider. The user logs in to the IdP portal (for example, the Okta dashboard or the Microsoft My Apps portal), sees a catalogue of available applications, and clicks on one. The IdP generates a SAML Response *without* having received an AuthnRequest from the SP, and posts it directly to the SP's ACS URL.

![Sequence diagram showing the IdP-Initiated SSO flow with numbered steps between Browser, Identity Provider, and Service Provider](/blog/deep-dive-saml-and-enterprise-federation/inline-3.png)

IdP-Initiated SSO Flow

### IdP-Initiated SSO — step by step

1.  **User authenticates with the IdP** — The user navigates to the IdP's portal and logs in (or already has an active session).
2.  **User selects the target application** — The user clicks on the SP's tile in the IdP's application catalogue.
3.  **IdP generates an unsolicited Response** — The IdP constructs a SAML Response with no `InResponseTo` attribute (since there was no AuthnRequest). The assertion is signed and sent to the SP's ACS URL via HTTP-POST.
4.  **SP validates the Response** — The SP validates the signature, conditions, and audience restriction. Since there is no `InResponseTo` to correlate, the SP relies on other validation checks.
5.  **SP establishes a local session** — The SP creates a session and redirects the user to the default landing page.

**Security note**: IdP-Initiated SSO is considered less secure than SP-Initiated SSO because the absence of an `InResponseTo` attribute makes it vulnerable to **unsolicited response replay attacks**. An attacker who captures a valid assertion could replay it to gain unauthorised access. For this reason, many security-conscious organisations disable IdP-Initiated SSO and require SP-Initiated flows exclusively.

## What is a SAML Assertion?

The **SAML Assertion** is the heart of the protocol. It is the XML document that carries the security claims — who the user is, how they authenticated, and what attributes they possess. An assertion contains up to three types of **statements**:

### AuthnStatement

The `<AuthnStatement>` confirms that the identity provider authenticated the principal. It includes:

*   **AuthnInstant** — the timestamp when authentication occurred
*   **SessionIndex** — a unique identifier for the IdP session (used for Single Logout)
*   **AuthnContext** — the method of authentication (e.g., password, MFA, X.509 certificate)

### AttributeStatement

The `<AttributeStatement>` carries zero or more `<Attribute>` elements — key-value pairs describing the user's properties. Common attributes include:

*   Email address
*   Display name
*   Group memberships
*   Roles
*   Department
*   Employee ID

### Conditions

The `<Conditions>` element constrains the validity of the assertion:

*   **NotBefore** and **NotOnOrAfter** — the time window during which the assertion is valid
*   **AudienceRestriction** — restricts which service providers may consume the assertion

Here is a complete assertion example:

```xml
<saml:Assertion
    xmlns:saml="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:assertion"
    Version="2.0"
    ID="_assertion_xyz789"
    IssueInstant="2025-07-12T09:30:05Z">
    <saml:Issuer>https://idp.example.com</saml:Issuer>
    <ds:Signature xmlns:ds="http://www.w3.org/2000/09/xmldsig#">
        <!-- XML Digital Signature (enveloped) -->
    </ds:Signature>
    <saml:Subject>
        <saml:NameID Format="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:nameid-format:emailAddress">
            jane.doe@example.com
        </saml:NameID>
        <saml:SubjectConfirmation Method="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:cm:bearer">
            <saml:SubjectConfirmationData
                InResponseTo="_8e8dc5f69a98cc4c1ff3427e5ce34606fd672f91e6"
                Recipient="https://app.example.com/saml/acs"
                NotOnOrAfter="2025-07-12T09:35:05Z"/>
        </saml:SubjectConfirmation>
    </saml:Subject>
    <saml:Conditions
        NotBefore="2025-07-12T09:29:55Z"
        NotOnOrAfter="2025-07-12T09:35:05Z">
        <saml:AudienceRestriction>
            <saml:Audience>https://app.example.com/saml/metadata</saml:Audience>
        </saml:AudienceRestriction>
    </saml:Conditions>
    <saml:AuthnStatement
        AuthnInstant="2025-07-12T09:30:03Z"
        SessionIndex="_session_abc123">
        <saml:AuthnContext>
            <saml:AuthnContextClassRef>
                urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:ac:classes:PasswordProtectedTransport
            </saml:AuthnContextClassRef>
        </saml:AuthnContext>
    </saml:AuthnStatement>
    <saml:AttributeStatement>
        <saml:Attribute Name="email"
            NameFormat="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:attrname-format:basic">
            <saml:AttributeValue>jane.doe@example.com</saml:AttributeValue>
        </saml:Attribute>
        <saml:Attribute Name="displayName"
            NameFormat="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:attrname-format:basic">
            <saml:AttributeValue>Jane Doe</saml:AttributeValue>
        </saml:Attribute>
        <saml:Attribute Name="groups"
            NameFormat="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:attrname-format:basic">
            <saml:AttributeValue>Engineering</saml:AttributeValue>
            <saml:AttributeValue>Platform-Admin</saml:AttributeValue>
        </saml:Attribute>
        <saml:Attribute Name="role"
            NameFormat="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:attrname-format:basic">
            <saml:AttributeValue>admin</saml:AttributeValue>
        </saml:Attribute>
    </saml:AttributeStatement>
</saml:Assertion>
```

**Key observation**: The assertion is signed using an **enveloped XML digital signature**. The SP verifies this signature using the IdP's public X.509 certificate (obtained from metadata). If the signature is invalid or the certificate does not match, the SP must reject the assertion entirely.

## What are SAML Bindings?

**SAML Bindings** define how SAML protocol messages (AuthnRequests, Responses, LogoutRequests) are transported between the IdP and SP over standard communication protocols. The SAML 2.0 specification defines several bindings, but three are by far the most commonly used:

| Binding | Transport | Direction | Message Size | Use Case |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **HTTP-Redirect** | URL query string (GET) | SP → IdP | Small (< 2KB due to URL limits) | AuthnRequests, LogoutRequests |
| **HTTP-POST** | HTML form (POST) | IdP → SP | Large (no practical limit) | SAML Responses with signed assertions |
| **HTTP-Artifact** | Small reference token + back-channel SOAP | Both | Tiny token; full message via back-channel | Highly sensitive environments |

### HTTP-Redirect Binding

The most common binding for sending AuthnRequests from the SP to the IdP. The XML message is deflated (zlib), Base64-encoded, and URL-encoded, then appended to the IdP's SSO URL as a query parameter:

```bash
https://idp.example.com/saml2/sso?SAMLRequest=fZJNT8MwDIb%2FSpR7...&RelayState=%2Fdashboard&SigAlg=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.w3.org%2F2001%2F04%2Fxmldsig-more%23rsa-sha256&Signature=abc123...
```

The `RelayState` parameter preserves the originally requested URL so the SP can redirect the user there after authentication.

### HTTP-POST Binding

The most common binding for delivering SAML Responses from the IdP to the SP. The IdP returns an HTML page containing a form with the Base64-encoded Response in a hidden field. JavaScript auto-submits the form:

```html
<html>
<body onload="document.forms[0].submit()">
    <form method="POST" action="https://app.example.com/saml/acs">
        <input type="hidden" name="SAMLResponse" value="PHNhbWxwOl..." />
        <input type="hidden" name="RelayState" value="/dashboard" />
        <noscript>
            <button type="submit">Continue</button>
        </noscript>
    </form>
</body>
</html>
```

### HTTP-Artifact Binding

In high-security environments, organisations may prefer not to pass the full SAML assertion through the user's browser. The **HTTP-Artifact** binding addresses this by sending a small opaque reference (the artifact) through the browser, and then the SP resolves the full assertion by making a direct back-channel SOAP call to the IdP's Artifact Resolution Service. This prevents the assertion from ever being exposed to the browser or to any intermediary.

## What are SAML Profiles?

**SAML Profiles** define how the assertions, protocols, and bindings are combined to achieve specific use cases. Profiles are essentially recipes — they prescribe which messages to use, which bindings to transport them, and what validation rules to apply. The key profiles are:

### Web Browser SSO Profile

This is the profile that implements the SP-Initiated and IdP-Initiated SSO flows we discussed earlier. It is by far the most widely implemented profile and the one you will encounter in virtually every enterprise SAML integration. The profile specifies:

*   The `<AuthnRequest>` must be sent via HTTP-Redirect or HTTP-POST
*   The `<Response>` must be sent via HTTP-POST
*   The assertion must contain a `<Subject>` with bearer confirmation
*   The `<Conditions>` must include an `<AudienceRestriction>`

### Single Logout (SLO) Profile

This profile handles the coordinated termination of sessions across all service providers when a user logs out. We will cover this in detail in a later section.

### Enhanced Client or Proxy (ECP) Profile

Designed for non-browser clients (mobile apps, desktop applications, API clients) that cannot follow HTTP redirects. The ECP profile uses SOAP and PAOS (Reverse SOAP) bindings to enable SAML SSO for these clients.

### Assertion Query/Request Profile

Allows a service provider to directly query the IdP for assertions about a user via a back-channel SOAP request, without involving the user's browser.

## How do you configure federation?

Establishing a SAML federation between an IdP and an SP involves three main steps: **metadata exchange**, **certificate trust**, and **attribute mapping**. Let's walk through each.

### Step 1 — Metadata exchange

Both the IdP and SP publish an XML metadata document that describes their SAML capabilities. Here is a simplified SP metadata document:

```xml
<md:EntityDescriptor
    xmlns:md="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:metadata"
    entityID="https://app.example.com/saml/metadata">
    <md:SPSSODescriptor
        AuthnRequestsSigned="true"
        WantAssertionsSigned="true"
        protocolSupportEnumeration="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:protocol">
        <md:KeyDescriptor use="signing">
            <ds:KeyInfo xmlns:ds="http://www.w3.org/2000/09/xmldsig#">
                <ds:X509Data>
                    <ds:X509Certificate>MIIC8jCCAdqgAwIB...</ds:X509Certificate>
                </ds:X509Data>
            </ds:KeyInfo>
        </md:KeyDescriptor>
        <md:KeyDescriptor use="encryption">
            <ds:KeyInfo xmlns:ds="http://www.w3.org/2000/09/xmldsig#">
                <ds:X509Data>
                    <ds:X509Certificate>MIIC8jCCAdqgAwIB...</ds:X509Certificate>
                </ds:X509Data>
            </ds:KeyInfo>
        </md:KeyDescriptor>
        <md:NameIDFormat>urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:nameid-format:emailAddress</md:NameIDFormat>
        <md:AssertionConsumerService
            Binding="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:bindings:HTTP-POST"
            Location="https://app.example.com/saml/acs"
            index="0"
            isDefault="true"/>
        <md:SingleLogoutService
            Binding="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:bindings:HTTP-Redirect"
            Location="https://app.example.com/saml/slo"/>
    </md:SPSSODescriptor>
</md:EntityDescriptor>
```

In practice, most IdPs and SPs provide a metadata URL that the counterpart can import. For example, Azure Entra ID exposes its metadata at:

```bash
https://login.microsoftonline.com/{tenant-id}/federationmetadata/2007-06/federationmetadata.xml
```

And Okta exposes its metadata at:

```bash
https://{your-domain}.okta.com/app/{app-id}/sso/saml/metadata
```

### Step 2 — Certificate trust

SAML security relies on X.509 certificates. The IdP signs assertions with its private key, and the SP verifies them with the IdP's public certificate (obtained from metadata). You can inspect a certificate using OpenSSL:

```bash
# Download the IdP's signing certificate from metadata
curl -s "https://idp.example.com/saml/metadata" \
  | xmllint --xpath "//*[local-name()='X509Certificate']/text()" - \
  | base64 -d > idp-cert.der

# Convert to PEM and inspect
openssl x509 -inform DER -in idp-cert.der -out idp-cert.pem
openssl x509 -in idp-cert.pem -text -noout
```

### Step 3 — Attribute mapping

Once the trust is established, you need to map the attributes in the SAML assertion to the fields your application expects. This mapping is typically configured in the SP's SAML settings:

```typescript
// Example attribute mapping configuration (TypeScript)
interface SamlAttributeMapping {
  email: string;
  displayName: string;
  groups: string;
  role: string;
}

const attributeMapping: SamlAttributeMapping = {
  email: "http://schemas.xmlsoap.org/ws/2005/05/identity/claims/emailaddress",
  displayName: "http://schemas.xmlsoap.org/ws/2005/05/identity/claims/name",
  groups: "http://schemas.xmlsoap.org/claims/Group",
  role: "http://schemas.microsoft.com/ws/2008/06/identity/claims/role",
};

function extractUserFromAssertion(
  attributes: Record<string, string[]>,
  mapping: SamlAttributeMapping
): { email: string; name: string; groups: string[]; role: string } {
  return {
    email: attributes[mapping.email]?.[0] ?? "",
    name: attributes[mapping.displayName]?.[0] ?? "",
    groups: attributes[mapping.groups] ?? [],
    role: attributes[mapping.role]?.[0] ?? "viewer",
  };
}
```

## How does SAML compare to OAuth 2.0 and OIDC?

One of the most common questions in identity architecture is: *when should I use SAML versus OAuth 2.0 versus OpenID Connect?* Here is a detailed comparison:

| Aspect | SAML 2.0 | OAuth 2.0 | OpenID Connect (OIDC) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Purpose** | Authentication + authorisation | Authorisation only | Authentication (built on OAuth 2.0) |
| **Token format** | XML assertion | Opaque or JWT access token | JWT ID token + access token |
| **Transport** | HTTP-Redirect, HTTP-POST, SOAP | HTTPS (REST) | HTTPS (REST) |
| **Data format** | XML | JSON | JSON |
| **Signature** | XML Digital Signature (XMLDSig) | JWS (JSON Web Signature) | JWS (JSON Web Signature) |
| **Encryption** | XML Encryption | JWE (JSON Web Encryption) | JWE (JSON Web Encryption) |
| **Discovery** | Metadata XML | RFC 8414 (Authorization Server Metadata) | OpenID Discovery (`/.well-known/openid-configuration`) |
| **Primary use case** | Enterprise SSO, B2B federation | API authorisation, mobile apps | Consumer SSO, modern web apps |
| **Complexity** | High (XML, signatures, bindings) | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Mobile/SPA support** | Poor (relies on browser redirects) | Excellent (PKCE, device flow) | Excellent |
| **Adoption** | Enterprise/legacy dominant | Universal | Growing rapidly |

**Guidance**: Use SAML for enterprise B2B federation where your partners mandate it. Use OIDC for new greenfield applications, consumer-facing identity, and mobile/SPA architectures. Use OAuth 2.0 (without OIDC) for pure API authorisation where you already know who the user is.

## What is enterprise federation at scale?

In small organisations, federation is straightforward — you have one IdP and a handful of SPs. But in large enterprises and multi-tenant SaaS platforms, the challenge grows significantly. Consider a SaaS provider that serves 500 enterprise customers, each with their own IdP. Configuring 500 individual SAML trust relationships is unmanageable. This is where **identity broker patterns** come in.

### Hub-and-spoke federation

In the **hub-and-spoke** model, a central identity broker sits between the service providers and the identity providers. The SPs only need to trust the broker (one trust relationship), and the broker maintains trust relationships with all the IdPs. The broker translates between them — accepting an assertion from a customer's IdP and issuing a new assertion to the SP.

![Diagram showing the hub-and-spoke identity broker pattern with multiple IdPs on one side, the broker in the centre, and multiple SPs on the other side](/blog/deep-dive-saml-and-enterprise-federation/inline-4.png)

Hub-and-Spoke Identity Broker Pattern

This pattern offers several advantages:

*   **Simplified SP configuration** — each SP only trusts the broker, regardless of how many IdPs exist
*   **Protocol translation** — the broker can accept SAML from one IdP and emit OIDC to an SP (or vice versa)
*   **Centralised policy enforcement** — the broker can apply consistent access policies, step-up authentication, and attribute transformation
*   **Tenant isolation** — multi-tenant SaaS platforms can route each tenant to their own IdP transparently

### Identity broker pattern in practice

Popular identity brokers include:

*   **Keycloak** — open-source identity broker supporting SAML and OIDC brokering
*   **Azure Entra External ID** — Microsoft's B2B federation service
*   **Auth0 / Okta** — enterprise SAML connections with automatic provisioning
*   **AWS IAM Identity Center** — federation for AWS multi-account environments

## How do you implement SAML?

Let's look at practical implementation examples in two popular languages.

### Node.js with passport-saml

The `@node-saml/passport-saml` library is the most widely used SAML library in the Node.js ecosystem. Here is a complete Express.js configuration:

```typescript
import express from "express";
import passport from "passport";
import {
  Strategy as SamlStrategy,
  type Profile,
  type VerifiedCallback,
} from "@node-saml/passport-saml";
import session from "express-session";
import fs from "fs";

const app = express();

app.use(
  session({
    secret: process.env.SESSION_SECRET!,
    resave: false,
    saveUninitialized: false,
    cookie: { secure: true, httpOnly: true, maxAge: 3600000 },
  })
);

app.use(passport.initialize());
app.use(passport.session());

const samlStrategy = new SamlStrategy(
  {
    callbackUrl: "https://app.example.com/saml/acs",
    entryPoint: "https://idp.example.com/saml2/sso",
    issuer: "https://app.example.com/saml/metadata",
    cert: fs.readFileSync("./certs/idp-signing.pem", "utf-8"),
    privateKey: fs.readFileSync("./certs/sp-private.pem", "utf-8"),
    decryptionPvk: fs.readFileSync("./certs/sp-encryption-private.pem", "utf-8"),
    signatureAlgorithm: "sha256",
    digestAlgorithm: "sha256",
    wantAssertionsSigned: true,
    wantAuthnResponseSigned: true,
    audience: "https://app.example.com/saml/metadata",
    maxAssertionAgeMs: 300000, // 5 minutes
  },
  (profile: Profile | null, done: VerifiedCallback) => {
    if (!profile) {
      return done(new Error("No SAML profile returned"));
    }
    const user = {
      id: profile.nameID,
      email: profile["email"] as string,
      displayName: profile["displayName"] as string,
      groups: (profile["groups"] as string[]) ?? [],
      sessionIndex: profile.sessionIndex,
    };
    return done(null, user);
  },
  (profile: Profile | null, done: VerifiedCallback) => {
    // Logout callback
    return done(null, profile);
  }
);

passport.use("saml", samlStrategy);

passport.serializeUser((user, done) => done(null, user));
passport.deserializeUser((user, done) => done(null, user as Express.User));

// SP-Initiated SSO: redirect to IdP
app.get("/login", passport.authenticate("saml", { failureRedirect: "/" }));

// Assertion Consumer Service (ACS): receive SAML Response
app.post(
  "/saml/acs",
  passport.authenticate("saml", {
    failureRedirect: "/login/failed",
  }),
  (req, res) => {
    const relayState = req.body.RelayState || "/dashboard";
    res.redirect(relayState);
  }
);

// SP Metadata endpoint
app.get("/saml/metadata", (req, res) => {
  res.type("application/xml");
  res.send(samlStrategy.generateServiceProviderMetadata(
    fs.readFileSync("./certs/sp-encryption.pem", "utf-8"),
    fs.readFileSync("./certs/sp-signing.pem", "utf-8")
  ));
});

app.listen(3000, () => console.log("SP running on port 3000"));
```

### Python with python3-saml

The `python3-saml` library by OneLogin provides a clean, well-documented API for SAML integration in Python applications. Here is a Flask example:

```python
from flask import Flask, request, redirect, session, make_response
from onelogin.saml2.auth import OneLogin_Saml2_Auth
from onelogin.saml2.utils import OneLogin_Saml2_Utils
import json

app = Flask(__name__)
app.secret_key = "your-secret-key"


def prepare_flask_request(req):
    """Convert Flask request to the format python3-saml expects."""
    url_data = req.url.split("?")
    return {
        "https": "on" if req.scheme == "https" else "off",
        "http_host": req.host,
        "server_port": req.environ.get("SERVER_PORT", "443"),
        "script_name": req.path,
        "get_data": req.args.copy(),
        "post_data": req.form.copy(),
        "query_string": req.query_string.decode("utf-8"),
    }


def init_saml_auth(req):
    """Initialise the SAML auth object with settings."""
    return OneLogin_Saml2_Auth(req, custom_base_path="./saml/")


@app.route("/login")
def login():
    """SP-Initiated SSO: redirect to the IdP."""
    req = prepare_flask_request(request)
    auth = init_saml_auth(req)
    return redirect(auth.login(return_to="/dashboard"))


@app.route("/saml/acs", methods=["POST"])
def acs():
    """Assertion Consumer Service: receive and validate the SAML Response."""
    req = prepare_flask_request(request)
    auth = init_saml_auth(req)
    auth.process_response()
    errors = auth.get_errors()

    if errors:
        return f"SAML Error: {', '.join(errors)}", 400

    if not auth.is_authenticated():
        return "Authentication failed", 401

    # Extract user attributes from the assertion
    session["saml_user"] = {
        "name_id": auth.get_nameid(),
        "session_index": auth.get_session_index(),
        "attributes": auth.get_attributes(),
    }

    relay_state = request.form.get("RelayState", "/dashboard")
    return redirect(relay_state)


@app.route("/saml/metadata")
def metadata():
    """Serve the SP metadata XML."""
    req = prepare_flask_request(request)
    auth = init_saml_auth(req)
    settings = auth.get_settings()
    sp_metadata = settings.get_sp_metadata()
    errors = settings.validate_metadata(sp_metadata)

    if errors:
        return f"Metadata Error: {', '.join(errors)}", 500

    resp = make_response(sp_metadata, 200)
    resp.headers["Content-Type"] = "application/xml"
    return resp


if __name__ == "__main__":
    app.run(port=5000, ssl_context="adhoc")
```

## What about Single Logout?

**Single Logout (SLO)** is the process of terminating a user's session across all federated service providers when they log out from any one of them. SLO is conceptually straightforward but operationally complex — it requires every SP in the federation to participate, and any single failure can leave orphaned sessions.

![Sequence diagram showing the Single Logout flow with numbered steps between Browser, Service Provider A, Identity Provider, and Service Provider B](/blog/deep-dive-saml-and-enterprise-federation/inline-5.png)

Single Logout (SLO) Flow

### SLO — step by step

1.  **User initiates logout at SP-A** — The user clicks "Logout" in SP-A's interface.
2.  **SP-A sends a LogoutRequest to the IdP** — SP-A constructs a `<LogoutRequest>` containing the user's `NameID` and `SessionIndex`, signs it, and sends it to the IdP's SLO endpoint via HTTP-Redirect.
3.  **IdP terminates its own session** — The IdP invalidates its local session for the user.
4.  **IdP sends LogoutRequests to other SPs** — The IdP iterates through all SPs that have active sessions for this user and sends a `<LogoutRequest>` to each (e.g., SP-B, SP-C).
5.  **Each SP terminates its session** — SP-B and SP-C invalidate their local sessions and respond with `<LogoutResponse>` messages.
6.  **IdP sends LogoutResponse to SP-A** — Once all SPs have responded (or timed out), the IdP sends a `<LogoutResponse>` back to SP-A.
7.  **SP-A confirms logout** — SP-A displays a "You have been logged out" page.

Here is a LogoutRequest example:

```xml
<samlp:LogoutRequest
    xmlns:samlp="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:protocol"
    xmlns:saml="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:assertion"
    ID="_logout_request_001"
    Version="2.0"
    IssueInstant="2025-07-12T10:00:00Z"
    Destination="https://idp.example.com/saml2/slo">
    <saml:Issuer>https://app.example.com/saml/metadata</saml:Issuer>
    <saml:NameID Format="urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:nameid-format:emailAddress">
        jane.doe@example.com
    </saml:NameID>
    <samlp:SessionIndex>_session_abc123</samlp:SessionIndex>
</samlp:LogoutRequest>
```

**Why SLO is hard in practice**: SLO relies on back-channel or front-channel propagation to every SP. If any SP is unavailable, the logout propagation fails silently. Many organisations opt for **session timeout-based logout** (short-lived SP sessions that require re-authentication) rather than relying on SLO for complete session termination.

## What are the security considerations?

SAML's XML-based nature introduces a unique class of security vulnerabilities that are distinct from those found in JSON-based protocols. Let's examine the critical ones.

### XML Signature Wrapping (XSW) attacks

**XML Signature Wrapping** is the most dangerous class of SAML attacks. The attacker manipulates the XML structure of a signed SAML Response — moving the signed assertion to a different location in the document and inserting a malicious assertion in its place. If the SP's validation logic verifies the signature against the original (now relocated) assertion but extracts identity data from the malicious assertion, the attacker can impersonate any user.

The defence is rigorous:

*   **Always validate that the signed element is the element you extract data from** — use schema-aware XML parsing, not naive XPath
*   **Use well-maintained SAML libraries** that have been tested against the known XSW attack variants (there are at least eight documented variants)
*   **Never implement SAML validation from scratch** — the edge cases are subtle and the consequences of getting it wrong are severe

### Replay attacks

An attacker who captures a valid SAML assertion could replay it to gain access. Defences include:

*   **Enforce `NotOnOrAfter` conditions** — reject assertions that have expired
*   **Track consumed assertion IDs** — maintain a cache of recently consumed assertion IDs and reject duplicates
*   **Require `InResponseTo` validation** — match the response to a pending AuthnRequest (prevents unsolicited response replay)

```typescript
// Replay detection middleware (TypeScript)
const consumedAssertions = new Map<string, number>();

function isReplayAttack(assertionId: string, expiresAt: number): boolean {
  // Clean expired entries periodically
  const now = Date.now();
  for (const [id, expiry] of consumedAssertions) {
    if (expiry < now) consumedAssertions.delete(id);
  }

  if (consumedAssertions.has(assertionId)) {
    return true; // Already consumed — replay detected
  }

  consumedAssertions.set(assertionId, expiresAt);
  return false;
}
```

### Certificate rotation

SAML signing certificates expire. When an IdP rotates its certificate, all federated SPs must update their trust configuration. Poor certificate rotation practices are a leading cause of SAML federation outages. Best practices include:

*   **Publish both old and new certificates in metadata during the transition period** — most SAML libraries support multiple verification certificates
*   **Automate metadata refresh** — configure SPs to periodically fetch the IdP's metadata URL
*   **Monitor certificate expiry** — alert well before expiry (30, 14, and 7 days)

```bash
# Check certificate expiry date from IdP metadata
curl -s "https://idp.example.com/saml/metadata" \
  | xmllint --xpath "//*[local-name()='X509Certificate'][1]/text()" - \
  | base64 -d \
  | openssl x509 -inform DER -noout -enddate

# Output: notAfter=Jul 12 00:00:00 2026 GMT
```

### Additional hardening

*   **Always require signed assertions** (`wantAssertionsSigned: true`) — unsigned assertions can be tampered with
*   **Use SHA-256 or stronger** for signatures — reject SHA-1
*   **Enforce audience restriction** — reject assertions not intended for your SP
*   **Validate the `Destination` attribute** — ensure it matches your ACS URL exactly
*   **Enable assertion encryption** for sensitive attributes (e.g., national ID numbers, healthcare data)

## What is the SAML debugging toolkit?

When things go wrong in a SAML integration — and they will — you need the right tools. Here is the essential debugging toolkit:

### Decode a SAML Response from browser traffic

```bash
# Capture the SAMLResponse from browser DevTools (Network tab, POST to ACS)
# Decode the Base64-encoded response
echo "PHNhbWxwOlJlc3BvbnNlIC..." | base64 -d | xmllint --format -
```

### Validate an XML signature manually

```bash
# Verify the SAML Response signature with xmlsec1
xmlsec1 --verify \
  --trusted-pem idp-cert.pem \
  --id-attr:ID "urn:oasis:names:tc:SAML:2.0:assertion:Assertion" \
  saml-response.xml
```

### Browser extensions and online tools

*   **SAML-tracer** (Firefox/Chrome extension) — captures SAML messages in real time as they flow through the browser
*   **samltool.com** — OneLogin's online SAML toolkit for encoding/decoding, validating signatures, and generating metadata
*   **SAML Developer Tools** (Chrome extension) — decodes SAML messages directly in DevTools

### Common debugging checklist

When a SAML integration fails, work through this checklist systematically:

1.  **Clock skew** — Is the time on the SP synchronised with the IdP? A difference of even a few minutes can cause `NotBefore`/`NotOnOrAfter` validation failures.
2.  **Entity ID mismatch** — Does the `Issuer` in the assertion match the Entity ID configured in the SP? Does the audience restriction match?
3.  **ACS URL mismatch** — Does the `Destination` in the response match the SP's configured ACS URL exactly (including scheme and trailing slashes)?
4.  **Certificate mismatch** — Is the SP using the correct IdP signing certificate? Has the certificate been rotated?
5.  **NameID format mismatch** — Is the IdP sending the NameID in the format the SP expects (email, persistent, transient)?
6.  **Signature scope** — Is the assertion signed, the response signed, or both? Does the SP require assertion-level signatures?

## What are common SAML terminology definitions?

To consolidate your understanding, here is a glossary of the key terms we have covered:

### What is an Entity ID?

A globally unique identifier for a SAML entity (IdP or SP), typically expressed as a URL. It appears as the `entityID` attribute in metadata and as the `<Issuer>` element in messages.

### What is the Assertion Consumer Service (ACS)?

The endpoint on the service provider that receives and processes SAML Responses. This is the URL the IdP posts the signed assertion to.

### What is a NameID?

The identifier for the principal within a SAML assertion. Common formats include email address (`emailAddress`), persistent (an opaque identifier that remains stable across sessions), and transient (a one-time identifier for privacy-preserving federation).

### What is RelayState?

An opaque string that the SP sends to the IdP and the IdP echoes back. It is used to preserve the user's originally requested URL across the SSO redirect so the SP can redirect them there after authentication.

### What is a SAML Artifact?

A small, fixed-size reference token (typically 44 bytes, Base64-encoded) that the IdP sends through the browser in lieu of the full SAML assertion. The SP resolves the full assertion by making a back-channel SOAP call to the IdP's Artifact Resolution Service.

### What is metadata?

An XML document that describes a SAML entity's capabilities, endpoints, certificates, and supported bindings. Metadata is exchanged between IdPs and SPs to establish trust relationships. It is defined in the SAML 2.0 Metadata specification (saml-metadata-2.0-os).

### What is assertion encryption?

The process of encrypting the SAML assertion (or specific elements within it) using the SP's public key. The SP decrypts it with its private key. This ensures that assertion contents are not visible to the user's browser or any intermediary — even though the assertion passes through the browser in the HTTP-POST binding.

### What is a trust circle?

A group of organisations that have mutually agreed to honour each other's SAML assertions. Trust circles are typically governed by a federation agreement that specifies assurance levels, attribute schemas, and operational policies.
