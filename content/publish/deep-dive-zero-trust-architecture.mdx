## What is the traditional perimeter security model?

Before we can appreciate why Zero Trust exists, we need to understand the model it replaces.

The traditional approach to network security is often called the **castle-and-moat model**. The idea is straightforward: build a strong perimeter (the moat) around your internal network (the castle). Everything outside the perimeter is untrusted, and everything inside is implicitly trusted. Firewalls, VPN concentrators, and demilitarised zones (DMZs) form the defensive boundary, and once a user or device has passed through authentication at the edge, they are generally free to move laterally across internal systems.

This model made reasonable sense when organisations operated on-premises data centres with well-defined network boundaries. Employees sat inside corporate offices, servers lived in a single data centre, and the attack surface was relatively contained.

However, the castle-and-moat approach suffers from several critical weaknesses in modern environments:

*   **Lateral movement** — Once an attacker breaches the perimeter (through phishing, credential theft, or a compromised VPN), they inherit the implicit trust of the internal network. They can move laterally from system to system, escalating privileges and exfiltrating data with little resistance.
*   **Insider threats** — Malicious or compromised insiders already reside inside the trusted perimeter. The model provides no mechanism to constrain their access beyond coarse network segmentation.
*   **Cloud sprawl** — Modern organisations deploy workloads across multiple cloud providers, SaaS platforms, and edge locations. There is no single perimeter to defend. The network boundary has effectively dissolved.
*   **Remote and hybrid work** — Employees connect from home networks, coffee shops, and mobile devices. Forcing all traffic through a central VPN gateway creates bottlenecks and a single point of failure.
*   **Over-provisioned VPN access** — Traditional VPNs typically grant broad network-level access once connected, rather than limiting access to specific applications or resources.

## What is Zero Trust?

**Zero Trust** is a security model built on one foundational principle: **never trust, always verify**. No user, device, or network location is inherently trusted. Every access request — whether it originates from inside the corporate network or from the public internet — must be continuously authenticated, authorised, and validated before access is granted.

The three core tenets of Zero Trust are:

1.  **Never trust, always verify** — Authenticate and authorise every request based on all available data points including user identity, device health, location, service or workload, data classification, and anomalies.
2.  **Least privilege access** — Limit user and service access to only the resources required for the task at hand, using just-in-time (JIT) and just-enough-access (JEA) policies.
3.  **Assume breach** — Design systems with the assumption that a breach has already occurred. Minimise blast radius through microsegmentation, end-to-end encryption, and continuous monitoring.

### A brief history

The term "Zero Trust" was coined by **John Kindervag** at Forrester Research in **2010**. Kindervag argued that the traditional trust model — where internal network traffic is trusted by default — was fundamentally flawed and that organisations should instead verify every transaction.

In **2014**, Google published details of **BeyondCorp**, its internal implementation of Zero Trust principles. BeyondCorp shifted access controls from the network perimeter to individual users and devices, enabling Google employees to work securely from any location without a traditional VPN. The BeyondCorp papers became a landmark reference for the industry and demonstrated that Zero Trust was practical at massive scale.

In **2020**, the **National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)** published **Special Publication 800-207**, formalising the Zero Trust Architecture framework and defining its core logical components. This publication remains the most widely referenced standard for ZTA design.

## What does NIST 800-207 define?

NIST SP 800-207 establishes a reference architecture for Zero Trust with three core logical components that work together to make and enforce access decisions:

### Policy Engine (PE)

The **Policy Engine** is the brain of the Zero Trust architecture. It is responsible for making the ultimate decision to grant, deny, or revoke access to a resource. The PE consumes input from multiple data sources — identity stores, device inventories, threat intelligence feeds, activity logs, and compliance policies — to compute a trust score and render an access decision.

### Policy Administrator (PA)

The **Policy Administrator** acts on the decision made by the Policy Engine. It is responsible for establishing or shutting down the communication path between the subject (user or workload) and the resource. When the PE grants access, the PA instructs the Policy Enforcement Point to allow the session. When access is denied or revoked, the PA instructs the PEP to terminate the connection.

### Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)

The **Policy Enforcement Point** is the gatekeeper. It enables, monitors, and ultimately terminates connections between subjects and enterprise resources. The PEP is the only component through which all traffic must pass, making it the enforcement boundary of the Zero Trust architecture.

![Zero trust architecture components showing Policy Engine, Policy Administrator, and Policy Enforcement Point flow](/blog/deep-dive-zero-trust-architecture/inline-2.png)

Zero Trust Architecture Components (PEP, PA, PE)

The numbered flow below explains how a request traverses the NIST 800-207 architecture:

1.  A **subject** (user or workload) sends an access request to reach an **enterprise resource** (application, database, API).
2.  The request arrives at the **Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)**, which intercepts the request before it reaches the resource.
3.  The PEP forwards the request context — including identity claims, device posture, network location, and requested resource — to the **Policy Administrator (PA)**.
4.  The PA passes the request context to the **Policy Engine (PE)** for evaluation.
5.  The PE evaluates the request against all configured policies, trust algorithms, and external data sources (identity provider, device inventory, threat intelligence, SIEM, compliance engine).
6.  The PE returns an **access decision** (grant or deny) to the PA.
7.  If access is granted, the PA instructs the PEP to **establish a secure session** between the subject and the resource with the appropriate scope and duration.
8.  If access is denied, the PA instructs the PEP to **block the request** and return an appropriate error to the subject.
9.  Throughout the session, the PEP **continuously monitors** the connection. If the trust level changes (e.g., anomalous behaviour is detected), the PE can re-evaluate and the PA can instruct the PEP to terminate the session.

## How does a request flow through a Zero Trust environment?

To put the NIST components into a practical context, let's trace a real-world request through a Zero Trust environment.

![Network request flow through zero trust showing identity verification, policy check, microsegmentation, and access](/blog/deep-dive-zero-trust-architecture/inline-3.png)

Zero Trust Request Flow

1.  A developer on a personal laptop connects to the corporate identity provider and authenticates with their credentials and a hardware security key (MFA).
2.  The identity provider issues a short-lived token containing the user's identity claims, group memberships, and device posture assessment.
3.  The developer's request to access a production Kubernetes dashboard hits the **Policy Enforcement Point** (e.g., an identity-aware proxy or API gateway).
4.  The PEP extracts the token and forwards the request context to the **Policy Administrator**.
5.  The PA queries the **Policy Engine**, which evaluates: Is this user a member of the platform-engineering group? Is their device compliant (encrypted disk, up-to-date OS, EDR agent running)? Is the request coming from a recognised geo-location? Is the requested resource classified as sensitive?
6.  The PE returns a **grant** decision with scoped permissions (read-only access to the non-production namespace).
7.  The PA instructs the PEP to establish an **mTLS-encrypted session** between the developer and the Kubernetes API server, scoped to the non-production namespace.
8.  The developer can now view workloads in the non-production namespace. Any attempt to access the production namespace or perform write operations is denied at the PEP.
9.  After 15 minutes of inactivity, the session is automatically terminated and re-authentication is required.

## What is identity-centric security?

In a Zero Trust model, the **identity** becomes the new perimeter. Rather than relying on network location to determine trust, every access decision is anchored to a verified identity — whether that identity belongs to a human user, a service account, or a machine workload.

### Key pillars of identity-centric security

*   **Identity Provider (IdP)** — A centralised service (e.g., Okta, Azure AD / Entra ID, Google Workspace) that authenticates users and issues identity tokens. The IdP is the single source of truth for who someone is.
*   **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** — Requires two or more verification factors (something you know, something you have, something you are) to reduce the risk of credential compromise.
*   **Conditional access policies** — Rules that evaluate context (device health, location, risk score, time of day) before granting access. For example: "Allow access to production systems only from managed devices with an up-to-date EDR agent."
*   **Device trust** — The device itself becomes part of the trust equation. Managed devices with verified posture (disk encryption, OS patch level, endpoint detection and response) receive a higher trust score than unknown or non-compliant devices.
*   **Context-aware access** — Decisions are not binary. The system evaluates the full context of the request — who, what, where, when, and how — and may grant partial access, require step-up authentication, or deny access entirely.
*   **Just-in-time (JIT) access** — Temporary, time-bound access grants that automatically expire. Rather than granting standing privileges, users request access when needed and it is revoked when the task is complete.

The shift from network perimeter to identity perimeter means that a user sitting inside the corporate office receives no more implicit trust than a user connecting from a coffee shop. Both must authenticate, both must satisfy policy, and both receive only the minimum access required.

## What is microsegmentation?

**Microsegmentation** is the practice of dividing a network into small, isolated segments — often down to the individual workload level — and applying granular security policies to control traffic between them.

### How does it differ from traditional network segmentation?

Traditional network segmentation uses VLANs, subnets, and firewall rules to create broad zones (e.g., DMZ, application tier, database tier). While this reduces the attack surface compared to a flat network, it still permits lateral movement within each zone.

Microsegmentation takes this further by enforcing policies at the workload, container, or process level. Each service communicates only with the specific services it needs, and all other traffic is denied by default.

### Implementation approaches

*   **Cloud security groups and network ACLs** — In AWS, security groups can be attached to individual ENIs (Elastic Network Interfaces), allowing per-instance or per-container rules. GCP firewall rules support service account-based targeting, and Azure Network Security Groups can be applied at the subnet or NIC level.
*   **Service mesh** — Platforms like **Istio**, **Linkerd**, and **Consul Connect** implement microsegmentation at the application layer using sidecar proxies. Each service-to-service call is authenticated via mTLS and authorised against fine-grained policies.
*   **Kubernetes network policies** — Native Kubernetes NetworkPolicy resources (enforced by CNI plugins like Calico or Cilium) control pod-to-pod traffic at the cluster level.
*   **Host-based firewalls** — Tools like iptables, nftables, or Windows Firewall can enforce microsegmentation at the operating system level.

The goal is always the same: **minimise the blast radius** of any single compromise by ensuring that an attacker who gains access to one workload cannot freely communicate with others.

## What is Mutual TLS (mTLS)?

**Mutual TLS (mTLS)** extends the standard TLS handshake so that both the client and the server present certificates and verify each other's identity. In standard TLS, only the server proves its identity to the client. With mTLS, the client must also present a valid certificate, ensuring that both parties in the communication are who they claim to be.

### How does mTLS work?

1.  The **client** initiates a TLS connection to the **server**.
2.  The server presents its TLS certificate to the client.
3.  The client verifies the server's certificate against a trusted Certificate Authority (CA).
4.  The server requests the client's certificate.
5.  The client presents its TLS certificate to the server.
6.  The server verifies the client's certificate against the trusted CA.
7.  Both parties have now authenticated each other. An encrypted session is established.

### SPIFFE and SPIRE for workload identity

**SPIFFE** (Secure Production Identity Framework For Everyone) is an open standard that defines a way to identify software workloads in heterogeneous environments. Each workload receives a **SPIFFE ID** — a structured URI like `spiffe://production.example.com/payment-service` — and a corresponding **SVID** (SPIFFE Verifiable Identity Document), which is typically an X.509 certificate.

**SPIRE** (the SPIFFE Runtime Environment) is the reference implementation that automates the issuance, rotation, and validation of SVIDs. SPIRE handles certificate lifecycle management so that workloads never deal with long-lived secrets.

### Service mesh mTLS with Istio

Service meshes like **Istio** and **Linkerd** automate mTLS between services. Sidecar proxies (Envoy in Istio's case) handle certificate management, key rotation, and mTLS negotiation transparently — application code does not need to change.

![Service mesh mutual TLS authentication flow](/blog/deep-dive-zero-trust-architecture/inline-5.png)

Service Mesh mTLS Flow

1.  **Service A** sends a request to **Service B**. The request is intercepted by Service A's sidecar proxy.
2.  The sidecar proxy initiates an **mTLS handshake** with Service B's sidecar proxy.
3.  Both proxies present and verify each other's **X.509 certificates** (issued by Istio's certificate authority, Istiod).
4.  Once mutual authentication is complete, the request is forwarded over the **encrypted channel** to Service B.
5.  Service B's sidecar proxy evaluates the request against configured **AuthorisationPolicies** before forwarding it to the application container.

To enforce strict mTLS across an entire namespace in Istio, apply the following `PeerAuthentication` resource:

```yaml
apiVersion: security.istio.io/v1beta1
kind: PeerAuthentication
metadata:
  name: default
  namespace: production
spec:
  mtls:
    mode: STRICT
```

You can verify mTLS is active between services by inspecting the Envoy proxy metrics:

```bash
istioctl x describe pod <pod-name> -n production
```

And to verify that a specific service-to-service connection is using mTLS:

```bash
istioctl authn tls-check <pod-name>.production <destination-service>.production.svc.cluster.local
```

### Zero Trust in AWS

AWS provides a comprehensive set of services for implementing Zero Trust across identity, network, and workload layers.

#### IAM and least privilege

AWS Identity and Access Management (IAM) is the foundation of access control in AWS. A core Zero Trust practice is to enforce **least privilege** — granting only the permissions required for a specific task.

Here is a sample IAM policy that grants read-only access to a single S3 bucket:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowReadOnlyAccessToReportsBucket",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": [
        "s3:GetObject",
        "s3:ListBucket"
      ],
      "Resource": [
        "arn:aws:s3:::company-reports-prod",
        "arn:aws:s3:::company-reports-prod/*"
      ],
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "aws:PrincipalTag/team": "finance"
        },
        "Bool": {
          "aws:MultiFactorAuthPresent": "true"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Note the **conditions**: access is only granted when the requesting principal has the `team=finance` tag and has authenticated with MFA. This is Zero Trust in action — identity plus context drives the access decision.

Create and attach this policy using the AWS CLI:

```bash
aws iam create-policy \
  --policy-name least-privilege-s3-reports \
  --policy-document file://policy.json \
  --description "Least privilege read-only access to reports bucket for finance team"

aws iam attach-role-policy \
  --role-name finance-read-role \
  --policy-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:policy/least-privilege-s3-reports
```

#### VPC and network isolation

AWS Virtual Private Cloud (VPC) provides the network isolation layer. In a Zero Trust model, you combine VPC constructs with identity-aware controls:

*   **Private subnets** — Place workloads in private subnets with no direct internet access.
*   **VPC Endpoints (PrivateLink)** — Access AWS services over private network paths, eliminating the need for traffic to traverse the public internet.
*   **Security groups** — Act as stateful firewalls at the instance/ENI level. Apply the principle of least privilege to network rules.

```bash
aws ec2 describe-vpc-endpoints \
  --filters "Name=service-name,Values=com.amazonaws.ap-southeast-2.s3"

aws ec2 create-vpc-endpoint \
  --vpc-id vpc-0abc123def456 \
  --service-name com.amazonaws.ap-southeast-2.s3 \
  --route-table-ids rtb-0abc123def456
```

#### AWS Verified Access

**AWS Verified Access** provides secure access to corporate applications without a VPN. It evaluates each request against identity and device posture policies before granting access — a direct implementation of the Zero Trust model.

#### Infrastructure as Code with CDK

Here is a TypeScript AWS CDK example that creates a VPC with private subnets and an S3 gateway endpoint — a foundational Zero Trust network pattern:

```typescript
import * as cdk from 'aws-cdk-lib';
import * as ec2 from 'aws-cdk-lib/aws-ec2';
import { Construct } from 'constructs';

export class ZeroTrustVpcStack extends cdk.Stack {
  constructor(scope: Construct, id: string, props?: cdk.StackProps) {
    super(scope, id, props);

    const vpc = new ec2.Vpc(this, 'ZeroTrustVpc', {
      maxAzs: 3,
      natGateways: 1,
      subnetConfiguration: [
        {
          cidrMask: 24,
          name: 'Private',
          subnetType: ec2.SubnetType.PRIVATE_WITH_EGRESS,
        },
        {
          cidrMask: 24,
          name: 'Isolated',
          subnetType: ec2.SubnetType.PRIVATE_ISOLATED,
        },
      ],
    });

    vpc.addGatewayEndpoint('S3Endpoint', {
      service: ec2.GatewayVpcEndpointAwsService.S3,
      subnets: [{ subnetType: ec2.SubnetType.PRIVATE_ISOLATED }],
    });
  }
}
```

#### Monitoring and threat detection

*   **AWS Security Hub** — Aggregates security findings from GuardDuty, Inspector, Macie, and third-party tools into a single pane. Provides automated compliance checks against frameworks like CIS and NIST.
*   **Amazon GuardDuty** — Continuous threat detection that monitors for malicious activity and anomalous behaviour across AWS accounts, workloads, and data.
*   **AWS CloudTrail** — Logs every API call made in your account, providing the audit trail essential for Zero Trust's "assume breach" posture.

```bash
aws guardduty list-detectors
aws securityhub get-findings --filters '{"SeverityLabel":[{"Value":"CRITICAL","Comparison":"EQUALS"}]}'
```

### Zero Trust in GCP

Google's **BeyondCorp** initiative is arguably the origin of modern enterprise Zero Trust. GCP offers first-party services that bring BeyondCorp principles to any organisation.

#### BeyondCorp Enterprise

**BeyondCorp Enterprise** provides a comprehensive Zero Trust platform that includes threat and data protection, identity-aware access, and endpoint verification. It integrates natively with Chrome Enterprise and Google Workspace.

#### Identity-Aware Proxy (IAP)

**Identity-Aware Proxy** is a managed service that controls access to cloud applications based on user identity and request context — without a VPN. IAP sits in front of your application and verifies that the requesting user is authenticated and authorised before allowing the connection through.

Enable IAP for a backend service:

```bash
gcloud compute backend-services update my-backend-service \
  --iap=enabled \
  --global

gcloud iap web add-iam-policy-binding \
  --resource-type=backend-services \
  --service=my-backend-service \
  --member="group:platform-engineers@example.com" \
  --role="roles/iap.httpsResourceAccessor"
```

#### VPC Service Controls

**VPC Service Controls** create security perimeters around Google Cloud resources to mitigate data exfiltration risks. They restrict which services, projects, and identities can access resources within the perimeter.

```bash
gcloud access-context-manager perimeters create production-perimeter \
  --title="Production Perimeter" \
  --resources="projects/123456789" \
  --restricted-services="storage.googleapis.com,bigquery.googleapis.com" \
  --policy=my-access-policy
```

#### Binary Authorisation

**Binary Authorisation** ensures that only trusted container images are deployed to GKE clusters. It verifies that images have been signed by a trusted authority before allowing deployment — applying Zero Trust principles to the software supply chain.

```bash
gcloud container binauthz policy export > policy.yaml

gcloud container binauthz attestors create production-attestor \
  --attestation-authority-note=projects/my-project/notes/production-note \
  --attestation-authority-note-project=my-project
```

### Zero Trust in Azure

Microsoft has invested heavily in Zero Trust capabilities across Azure and the broader Microsoft 365 ecosystem.

#### Azure AD (Entra ID) and Conditional Access

**Microsoft Entra ID** (formerly Azure Active Directory) is the identity platform at the heart of Microsoft's Zero Trust strategy. **Conditional Access policies** evaluate signals such as user identity, device compliance, location, and real-time risk to make access decisions.

## What are the implementation patterns and maturity levels?

Implementing Zero Trust is a journey, not a single deployment. The following maturity model provides a framework for assessing where your organisation sits today and planning the path forward.

![Zero trust implementation maturity model from traditional to advanced](/blog/deep-dive-zero-trust-architecture/inline-6.png)

Implementation Maturity Model

### Level 1 — Traditional

*   Perimeter-based security with firewalls and VPNs
*   Implicit trust inside the network
*   Coarse-grained network segmentation (VLANs, broad firewall rules)
*   Static, long-lived credentials
*   Limited logging and monitoring

### Level 2 — Partial Zero Trust

*   MFA enforced for all human users
*   Identity-aware access for cloud applications (e.g., IAP, Verified Access)
*   Initial microsegmentation of critical workloads
*   Short-lived credentials and token-based authentication
*   Centralised logging with basic alerting
*   Device posture assessment begins

### Level 3 — Advanced Zero Trust

*   All access decisions are identity-centric and context-aware
*   Microsegmentation applied across all environments (cloud, on-premises, hybrid)
*   mTLS enforced for all service-to-service communication
*   Continuous monitoring with automated threat detection and response
*   Just-in-time (JIT) access for privileged operations
*   Software supply chain verification (image signing, SBOM)
*   Comprehensive audit trail across all systems

### Level 4 — Optimal Zero Trust

*   Fully automated policy enforcement with real-time adaptive controls
*   Machine learning-driven anomaly detection and risk scoring
*   Zero standing privileges — all access is JIT with automated approval workflows
*   Unified policy engine across all environments and cloud providers
*   Continuous compliance validation against industry frameworks (NIST, CIS, ISO 27001)
*   Red team exercises and breach simulation as standard practice

### Migration strategies

When migrating from a traditional perimeter model to Zero Trust, consider these practical strategies:

1.  **Start with identity** — Enforce MFA everywhere. Consolidate identity into a single provider. This is the highest-impact, lowest-risk starting point.
2.  **Map your data flows** — Understand how data moves between systems. You cannot protect what you cannot see.
3.  **Segment critical workloads first** — Apply microsegmentation to your most sensitive systems (databases, payment processing, PII stores) before broadening to all workloads.
4.  **Adopt a service mesh** — For microservices architectures, a service mesh provides mTLS, observability, and policy enforcement with minimal application changes.
5.  **Implement continuous monitoring** — Deploy a SIEM, enable cloud-native threat detection (GuardDuty, Security Command Center, Sentinel), and establish alerting pipelines.
6.  **Iterate and measure** — Use the maturity model to track progress. Conduct regular assessments and adjust policies based on findings.

## Okay, what terminology do I need to understand?

### What is a Policy Engine (PE)?

The **Policy Engine** is the decision-making component in a Zero Trust architecture. It evaluates access requests against configured policies, trust algorithms, and external data sources to produce a grant or deny decision.

### What is a Policy Administrator (PA)?

The **Policy Administrator** executes the decisions made by the Policy Engine. It establishes, modifies, or terminates communication paths between subjects and resources by instructing the Policy Enforcement Point.

### What is a Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)?

The **Policy Enforcement Point** is the inline component that intercepts all access requests. It enables, monitors, and terminates connections based on instructions from the Policy Administrator. All traffic between a subject and a resource must pass through the PEP.

### What is microsegmentation?

**Microsegmentation** is the practice of creating fine-grained security zones within an environment, typically down to the individual workload or container level. It restricts lateral movement by enforcing that each workload can only communicate with specifically authorised peers.

### What is mTLS?

**Mutual TLS (mTLS)** is an extension of the TLS protocol where both the client and the server authenticate each other using X.509 certificates. It ensures that both parties in a communication are verified, providing strong service-to-service authentication.

### What is SPIFFE?

**SPIFFE** (Secure Production Identity Framework For Everyone) is an open standard for securely identifying software workloads in dynamic and heterogeneous environments. It defines a structured identity format (SPIFFE ID) and verifiable identity documents (SVIDs).

### What is SPIRE?

**SPIRE** (SPIFFE Runtime Environment) is the reference implementation of the SPIFFE standard. It automates the issuance, rotation, and validation of workload identity documents, eliminating the need for long-lived secrets.

### What is lateral movement?

**Lateral movement** refers to an attacker's ability to move from one compromised system to other systems within the same network. In traditional perimeter models, once the boundary is breached, lateral movement is often trivially easy due to implicit trust.

### What is least privilege?

**Least privilege** is the principle of granting users, services, and processes only the minimum permissions required to perform their function. It reduces the potential damage from compromised accounts and limits the attack surface.

### What is an identity provider (IdP)?

An **identity provider** is a service that creates, maintains, and manages identity information for users, services, and devices. It authenticates subjects and issues identity tokens or assertions that other systems consume to make access decisions. Examples include Okta, Microsoft Entra ID, and Google Workspace.

### What is device trust?

**Device trust** is the practice of evaluating the security posture of a device (operating system version, disk encryption, endpoint detection agent, patch level) as part of the access decision. Devices that do not meet the required posture may be denied access or granted restricted access.

### What is BeyondCorp?

**BeyondCorp** is Google's implementation of Zero Trust principles. It shifts access controls from the network perimeter to individual users and devices, allowing employees to work securely from any network without a VPN. Google published the BeyondCorp research papers starting in 2014, and later commercialised the approach as BeyondCorp Enterprise.

### What is NIST 800-207?

**NIST Special Publication 800-207** is a document published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology that defines the Zero Trust Architecture framework. It describes the logical components (PE, PA, PEP), deployment models, and use cases for Zero Trust.

### What is a service mesh?

A **service mesh** is a dedicated infrastructure layer for managing service-to-service communication in a microservices architecture. It typically uses sidecar proxies to handle mTLS, traffic management, observability, and access control without requiring changes to application code. Examples include Istio, Linkerd, and Consul Connect.

### What is just-in-time (JIT) access?

**Just-in-time access** is a practice where elevated privileges are granted temporarily and automatically revoked after a defined period. It minimises the window of exposure by ensuring that users do not hold standing privileged access.
