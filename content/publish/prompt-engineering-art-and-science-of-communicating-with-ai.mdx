## What is prompt engineering?

At its simplest, **prompt engineering** is the practice of crafting inputs (prompts) to AI models in a way that produces the desired output. Think of it as learning a new communication skill — just as you would tailor how you brief a colleague depending on their expertise and the task at hand, you need to tailor how you communicate with an AI model.

A **prompt** can include:

*   **Instructions** — What you want the model to do
*   **Context** — Background information the model needs to understand the task
*   **Input data** — The specific content you want the model to process
*   **Output format** — How you want the response structured

The reason prompt engineering has become so important is that modern LLMs like GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, and Llama are incredibly capable, but they are also sensitive to how requests are framed. A small change in wording can produce dramatically different results.

## Why does prompt engineering matter?

If I'm to be perfectly honest with you, when I first started working with LLMs, I underestimated how much the prompt itself influenced the output. I would type a quick question and wonder why the response was vague or off-target. The reality is:

*   **Precision drives quality** — Vague prompts produce vague answers. Specific, well-structured prompts produce focused, actionable results.
*   **It saves time** — Getting the prompt right upfront means fewer iterations, less back-and-forth, and faster time to value.
*   **It unlocks capabilities** — Many of the most powerful things LLMs can do (reasoning, structured analysis, code generation) only emerge when prompted correctly.
*   **It is a transferable skill** — The principles of good prompt engineering apply across models and tools. Whether you are using ChatGPT, GitHub Copilot, Amazon Bedrock, or Google Vertex AI, the fundamentals remain the same.
*   **It is becoming a professional skill** — Organisations are increasingly recognising prompt engineering as a competency. Teams that prompt well outperform those that do not.

## What are the core prompting techniques?

Let's walk through the foundational techniques that every practitioner should understand.

**Zero-Shot Prompting**

This is the simplest technique — you ask the model to perform a task with no examples provided. You rely entirely on the model's pre-trained knowledge.

*   **When to use** — Simple, well-understood tasks where the model is likely to already know the expected format
*   **Example** — "Summarise the key benefits of microservices architecture in three bullet points."
*   **Strength** — Quick and easy; no preparation needed
*   **Limitation** — Less reliable for complex or ambiguous tasks

Difficulty to adopt: **1** out of 10

**Few-Shot Prompting**

Here you provide the model with a small number of examples demonstrating the desired input-output pattern before asking it to perform the task.

*   **When to use** — When you need the model to follow a specific format or reasoning style that is not obvious from the instruction alone
*   **Example** — "Here are three examples of how to classify customer feedback as positive, negative, or neutral: [examples]. Now classify the following feedback..."
*   **Strength** — Significantly improves consistency and accuracy
*   **Limitation** — Uses more tokens (and thus cost); examples must be representative

Difficulty to adopt: **2** out of 10

**Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting**

This technique instructs the model to reason step by step before arriving at an answer. It is particularly powerful for tasks requiring logic, mathematics, or multi-step reasoning.

*   **When to use** — Complex reasoning tasks, mathematical problems, decision-making scenarios
*   **Example** — "Think through this step by step: A company has 500 servers. If they migrate 20% to the cloud each quarter, how many quarters until all servers are migrated?"
*   **Strength** — Dramatically improves accuracy on reasoning tasks
*   **Limitation** — Produces longer responses; not necessary for simple tasks

Difficulty to adopt: **3** out of 10

**Self-Consistency Prompting**

An extension of chain-of-thought where you ask the model to generate multiple reasoning paths and then select the most consistent answer.

*   **When to use** — High-stakes decisions where you want to validate the model's reasoning
*   **Strength** — Reduces errors by cross-checking multiple approaches
*   **Limitation** — Significantly increases token usage and cost

Difficulty to adopt: **5** out of 10

## What are system prompts and why do they matter?

A **system prompt** (also known as a system message) is an instruction set at the beginning of a conversation that defines the model's behaviour, personality, constraints, and context. Unlike user prompts, the system prompt persists across the entire conversation.

System prompts are powerful because they allow you to:

*   **Set the persona** — "You are a senior cloud architect with 15 years of experience in AWS and GCP."
*   **Define constraints** — "Only provide answers based on the documentation I provide. Do not make assumptions."
*   **Establish format** — "Always respond with a brief summary followed by detailed bullet points."
*   **Control tone** — "Use a professional but approachable tone suitable for a technical blog."
*   **Enforce guardrails** — "Do not provide medical advice. If asked, redirect the user to consult a professional."

When building applications on top of LLMs, the system prompt is your primary mechanism for controlling model behaviour. It is where you encode your application's personality, rules, and boundaries.

## How do temperature and parameters affect output?

Beyond the prompt itself, model parameters significantly influence the output:

**Temperature**

*   Controls the randomness of the model's output
*   **Low temperature (0.0 – 0.3)** — More deterministic, focused, and consistent. Best for factual tasks, code generation, and structured output.
*   **Medium temperature (0.4 – 0.7)** — Balanced creativity and accuracy. Good for general-purpose tasks.
*   **High temperature (0.8 – 1.0+)** — More creative, diverse, and unpredictable. Best for brainstorming, creative writing, and exploration.

**Top-P (Nucleus Sampling)**

*   Controls the cumulative probability threshold for token selection
*   A top-p of 0.9 means the model considers tokens that make up the top 90% of probability mass
*   Lower values produce more focused output; higher values allow more diversity

**Max Tokens**

*   Sets the maximum length of the response
*   Important for controlling costs and ensuring responses fit within your application's constraints

**Frequency Penalty and Presence Penalty**

*   **Frequency penalty** — Reduces repetition by penalising tokens that have already appeared frequently
*   **Presence penalty** — Encourages the model to introduce new topics by penalising any token that has appeared at all

A practical tip: for most professional and technical use cases, start with a **temperature of 0.2–0.4** and a **top-p of 0.9**. Adjust from there based on your results.

## What are the most useful prompt patterns?

Here are practical patterns that I use regularly and recommend to teams:

**The Persona Pattern**

Tell the model who it is before asking the question. This primes the model to draw on relevant knowledge and adopt the appropriate communication style.

*   "You are a solutions architect reviewing a system design. Identify the three biggest risks in the following architecture..."

**The Template Pattern**

Provide a structured template for the model to fill in. This is excellent for generating consistent, formatted output.

*   "Using the following template, write a technical design document: Title: [title], Problem Statement: [problem], Proposed Solution: [solution], Risks: [risks], Timeline: [timeline]."

**The Chain Pattern**

Break a complex task into a sequence of smaller prompts, feeding the output of one into the next. This is particularly effective for multi-step workflows.

*   Step 1: "Extract the key requirements from this document."
*   Step 2: "Based on these requirements, propose three architecture options."
*   Step 3: "Evaluate these options against cost, scalability, and time-to-market."

**The Critique Pattern**

Ask the model to evaluate or critique its own output, then improve it.

*   "Review the response you just provided. Identify any weaknesses, gaps, or inaccuracies, then provide an improved version."

**The Constraint Pattern**

Set explicit boundaries on the response to focus the output.

*   "Explain Kubernetes networking in exactly five bullet points, each no longer than two sentences. Assume the reader is a mid-level developer."

## What are the common mistakes to avoid?

Having worked with prompt engineering across multiple projects and teams, these are the most frequent mistakes I see:

*   **Being too vague** — "Tell me about cloud" will produce a generic essay. "Compare the compute pricing models of AWS EC2, GCP Compute Engine, and Azure Virtual Machines for a workload running 24/7" will produce something useful.
*   **Not specifying the output format** — If you need JSON, a table, bullet points, or a specific structure, say so explicitly. Do not assume the model will guess correctly.
*   **Ignoring context length** — LLMs have finite context windows. Overloading a prompt with irrelevant information dilutes the quality of the response.
*   **Not iterating** — Your first prompt is rarely your best prompt. Treat prompt engineering as an iterative process. Refine based on the output.
*   **Assuming the model knows your context** — The model does not know your organisation, your codebase, or your constraints unless you tell it. Provide relevant context explicitly.
*   **Over-relying on a single prompt** — For complex tasks, break the work into multiple prompts rather than trying to do everything in one.
*   **Neglecting to test across inputs** — A prompt that works for one example may fail for others. Test with diverse inputs to ensure robustness.

## What tools can help with prompt engineering?

The tooling ecosystem around prompt engineering is growing rapidly:

*   **OpenAI Playground / API** — Test prompts with different models and parameters in real time
*   **Amazon Bedrock** — Provides access to multiple foundation models with a unified API, making it easy to compare prompt results across models
*   **Google Vertex AI Studio** — Visual prompt design and testing environment for Gemini and other models
*   **Azure AI Studio** — Integrated environment for prompt engineering with Azure OpenAI Service models
*   **LangChain / LangSmith** — Open-source framework for building LLM applications with built-in prompt management, chaining, and observability
*   **Prompt flow** — Microsoft's tool for building and evaluating prompt-based workflows
*   **GitHub Copilot** — While primarily a coding assistant, understanding how to prompt Copilot effectively is itself an exercise in prompt engineering

## Conclusion

Prompt engineering is rapidly becoming one of the most valuable skills in the AI era. Here are the key takeaways:

*   **Prompt engineering is the practice of crafting effective inputs** to get high-quality outputs from AI models — it is both an art and a science
*   **Core techniques include zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought, and self-consistency prompting**, each suited to different levels of task complexity
*   **System prompts are your control mechanism** for defining model behaviour, persona, constraints, and guardrails
*   **Model parameters like temperature and top-p** significantly influence output quality and should be tuned to your use case
*   **Practical patterns** — persona, template, chain, critique, and constraint — provide repeatable frameworks for common tasks
*   **Avoid common mistakes** like being too vague, not specifying format, and failing to iterate
*   **The tooling ecosystem is maturing**, with AWS, GCP, Azure, and open-source frameworks all providing prompt engineering capabilities

The most effective AI practitioners are not necessarily those with the deepest machine learning knowledge — they are the ones who have learned to communicate clearly and precisely with AI models. Invest in this skill, and you will see the returns across everything you build.
